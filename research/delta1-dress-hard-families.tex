\begin{abstract}
In this paper we study the single-deletion variant $\Delta^1$-DRESS, part of the broader DRESS framework.
We demonstrate empirically that $\Delta^1$-DRESS, a single level of vertex deletion applied to the DRESS graph fingerprint, achieves unique fingerprints within each tested SRG parameter family across all 51{,}718 non-isomorphic strongly regular graphs (SRGs) considered, spanning 16 parameter families: the complete Spence collection (12 families, 43{,}703 graphs on up to 64 vertices) plus four additional SRG families with up to 4{,}466 graphs per family.
Combined with 18 additional hard graph families (102 graphs including Miyazaki, Chang, Paley, Latin square, and Steiner constructions), $\Delta^1$-DRESS achieves 100\% within-family separation across 34 benchmark families covering 51{,}816 distinct graph instances, implicitly resolving over 576 million within-family non-isomorphic pairs.
Moreover, the classical Rook $L_2(4)$ vs.\ Shrikhande pair, SRG(16,6,2,2), is known to be indistinguishable by the original 3-WL algorithm~\cite{cai1992optimal}, yet $\Delta^1$-DRESS separates it~\cite{castrillo2026dress}, proving that $\Delta^1$-DRESS is \emph{strictly more powerful} than 3-WL.
The method runs in polynomial time $\mathcal{O}(n \cdot I \cdot m \cdot d_{\max})$ per graph; a streamed implementation of the combined fingerprint uses $\mathcal{O}(m + B + n)$ memory, where $B$ is the number of histogram bins, while the experiments reported here additionally retain the full deleted-subgraph multiset matrix for post-hoc analysis.
\end{abstract}


\section{Introduction}

The graph isomorphism problem (GI), deciding whether two graphs are structurally identical, occupies a singular position in computational complexity.
While Babai's breakthrough~\cite{babai2016graph} places GI in quasipolynomial time, practical algorithms face a stark gap: simple heuristics solve most instances instantly, but specifically constructed ``hard families'' remain challenging for all known polynomial-time methods.

The Weisfeiler--Leman (WL) hierarchy~\cite{weisfeiler1968reduction} provides the standard benchmark for isomorphism distinguishing power.
The $k$-WL algorithm refines colorings of $k$-tuples of vertices and runs in $\mathcal{O}(n^{k+1})$ time per iteration.
For $k=1$ (color refinement), this is practical at scale but fails on strongly regular graphs (SRGs).
For $k=2$, it handles some SRGs but fails on others.
For $k \geq 3$, the $\mathcal{O}(n^4)$ per-iteration cost makes large-scale evaluation impractical, and 3-WL does \emph{not} suffice for all SRG parameter sets: it is known to fail on SRG(16,6,2,2)~\cite{cai1992optimal}.

DRESS~\cite{castrillo2018dynamicstructuralsimilaritygraphs, castrillo2026dress} is a parameter-free dynamical system on graph edges that converges to a canonical fixed point at $\mathcal{O}(m \cdot d_{\max})$ cost per iteration, where $m = |E|$.
Original-DRESS is provably at least as expressive as 2-WL~\cite{castrillo2026dress}.
Its vertex-deletion extension, $\Delta^k$-DRESS, is \emph{conjectured} to be at least as powerful as $(k+2)$-WL~\cite{castrillo2026dress}, a conjecture supported by the CFI staircase experiments, where each deletion level empirically adds exactly one WL dimension of expressiveness.
In what follows, we use ``DRESS'' to denote the overall framework and focus specifically on its single-deletion member $\Delta^1$-DRESS.
Originally formulated as the DRESS--WL Dominance Conjecture in~\cite{castrillo2026dress}, the base case ($k=0$, Original-DRESS $\geq$ 2-WL) is proven; the inductive step ($k \geq 1$) remains open.

The central question of this paper is practical: \emph{is $k=1$ enough?}
Specifically, does $\Delta^1$-DRESS separate every hard benchmark family considered in this study?
We answer this affirmatively by testing $\Delta^1$-DRESS on the most comprehensive benchmark of hard graph isomorphism instances ever assembled:

\begin{itemize}
    \item The \textbf{complete Spence collection}~\cite{spence2024srg} of strongly regular graphs on up to 64 vertices: 12 parameter families totaling 43{,}703 graphs, including the massive SRG(36,15,6,6) family with 32{,}548 graphs.

    \item \textbf{Four additional SRG families} from McKay's online graph collections~\cite{mckaygraphs} and combinatorial design catalogues: SRG(45,22,10,11), two subfamilies of SRG(63,32,16,26), and SRG(65,32,15,16), totaling 8{,}015 graphs on up to 65 vertices.

    \item \textbf{18 constructed hard families}: 102 graphs including Miyazaki (CFI-type), Chang, Paley, Rook, Shrikhande, Latin square, Steiner, Kneser, Johnson, Hamming, and generalized quadrangle constructions.
\end{itemize}

\noindent \textbf{Result.}
$\Delta^1$-DRESS achieves \textbf{100\% within-family separation}: within each tested family, every graph receives a unique fingerprint.
No within-family collisions remain across the 34 benchmark families, accounting for over 576 million within-family non-isomorphic pairs.

\subsection{Contributions}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item We introduce a robust fingerprint for $\Delta^1$-DRESS based on the histogram plus the \emph{multiplicity signature}, an integer invariant extracted from repeated rows in the deleted-subgraph DRESS matrix (Section~\ref{sec:fingerprint}).

    \item We evaluate $\Delta^1$-DRESS on all 12 Spence SRG families (43{,}703 graphs) plus four additional SRG families (8{,}015 graphs), achieving 100\% within-family separation in each family (Section~\ref{sec:results}).
    Duda~\cite{duda2024simple} previously achieved full separation on the Spence dataset using ad hoc algebraic invariants; our work is the first to do so with a method that has both a proved WL lower bound for Original-DRESS and a demonstrated strict separation from 3-WL at deletion depth $1$.

    \item We evaluate $\Delta^1$-DRESS on 18 synthetically constructed hard families (102 graphs), again achieving 100\% within-family separation.

    \item Since 3-WL is known to fail on the Rook $L_2(4)$ vs.\ Shrikhande pair, SRG(16,6,2,2)~\cite{cai1992optimal}, and $\Delta^1$-DRESS separates it~\cite{castrillo2026dress}, $\Delta^1$-DRESS $>$ 3-WL (Section~\ref{sec:3wl-counterexample}).

    \item To our knowledge, we provide the first large-scale empirical evidence that a single level of vertex deletion is sufficient across every hard benchmark family tested in this study.
\end{enumerate}


\section{Background}

\subsection{DRESS}

The DRESS equation~\cite{castrillo2018dynamicstructuralsimilaritygraphs} is a parameter-free, non-linear dynamical system on edges.
Given a graph $G = (V, E)$ with self-loops added to all vertices, the update rule is:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:dress}
d_{uv}^{(t+1)} = \frac{\sum_{x \in N[u] \cap N[v]} \bigl(d_{ux}^{(t)} + d_{xv}^{(t)}\bigr)}{\|u\|^{(t)} \cdot \|v\|^{(t)}}
\end{equation}
where $\|u\|^{(t)} = \sqrt{\sum_{x \in N[u]} d_{ux}^{(t)}}$ is the vertex norm and $N[u] = N(u) \cup \{u\}$ is the closed neighborhood.
The system converges to a unique fixed point $d^* \in [0, 2]^{|E|}$ for any positive initialization~\cite{castrillo2026dress}; in the convergence proof of~\cite{castrillo2026dress}, this follows from Birkhoff contraction in the Hilbert projective metric.

The graph fingerprint is the histogram $h(d^*)$: each converged edge value $d_{uv}^*$ is mapped to an integer bin $\lfloor d_{uv}^* / \epsilon \rfloor$ (where $\epsilon$ is the convergence tolerance), and the histogram records the count of edges in each bin.
This fingerprint is a complete isomorphism invariant of the DRESS fixed point.


\subsection{\texorpdfstring{$\Delta^1$}{Delta-1}-DRESS}

$\Delta^1$-DRESS~\cite{castrillo2026dress} runs DRESS on each vertex-deleted subgraph and pools the results:

\begin{definition}[$\Delta^1$-DRESS]
\label{def:delta1}
Given a graph $G = (V, E)$, the $\Delta^1$-DRESS fingerprint is:
\[
\Delta^1\text{-DRESS}(G) = h\!\left(\bigsqcup_{v \in V} \text{DRESS}(G \setminus \{v\})\right)
\]
where $G \setminus \{v\}$ is the induced subgraph on $V \setminus \{v\}$, $\bigsqcup$ is multiset union, and $h$ maps the pooled edge values into a histogram.
\end{definition}

The pooled histogram contains $n$ contributions (one per vertex deletion), each yielding up to $\binom{n-1}{2}$ edge values.
The total cost per graph is $\mathcal{O}(n \cdot I \cdot m \cdot d_{\max})$, where $I$ is the number of DRESS iterations (typically $\leq 30$).

The DRESS--WL Dominance Conjecture~\cite{castrillo2026dress} posits that $\Delta^k$-DRESS $\geq$ $(k{+}2)$-WL for all $k \geq 0$.
The base case ($k = 0$, Original-DRESS $\geq$ 2-WL) is proved; the case $k \geq 1$ remains open.
Empirical evidence strongly supports the conjecture: on the CFI staircase~\cite{cai1992optimal}, $\Delta^1$-DRESS distinguishes $\text{CFI}(K_4)$ (requiring exactly 3-WL) while failing on $\text{CFI}(K_5)$ (which 4-WL distinguishes), and analogous staircase behavior was reported through $k = 3$ in~\cite{castrillo2026dress}.


\subsection{Strongly Regular Graphs}

A \emph{strongly regular graph} $\text{SRG}(n, d, \lambda, \mu)$ is a $d$-regular graph on $n$ vertices where every adjacent pair has $\lambda$ common neighbors and every non-adjacent pair has $\mu$ common neighbors.
SRGs are cospectral by construction: all graphs with the same parameters share the same spectrum.
This makes them immune to spectral methods and a canonical hard benchmark for isomorphism testing.

1-WL (color refinement) fails on all SRGs: the regular structure ensures a uniform initial coloring that never refines.
2-WL succeeds on some SRG families but fails on others.
3-WL does not suffice for all SRG parameter sets: it is known to fail on SRG(16,6,2,2)~\cite{cai1992optimal}.  Moreover, its $\mathcal{O}(n^4)$ per-iteration cost makes large-scale evaluation on the Spence collection computationally infeasible.

\section{The \texorpdfstring{$\Delta^1$}{Delta-1}-DRESS Fingerprint}
\label{sec:fingerprint}

The $\Delta^1$-DRESS computation produces, for each graph $G$ on $n$ vertices, a collection of $n$ DRESS fingerprint vectors, one per vertex deletion.
We combine this information into a two-part fingerprint designed for robustness against floating-point artifacts.


\subsection{Pooled Histogram}

The primary fingerprint channel is the \emph{pooled histogram}: the histogram $h$ of all edge values across all $n$ vertex deletions (Definition~\ref{def:delta1}).
Each converged edge value $d_{uv}^*$ is quantized to bin $\lfloor d_{uv}^* / \epsilon \rfloor$ where $\epsilon = 10^{-6}$ is the convergence tolerance.
The histogram is a vector of non-negative integers, exact, deterministic, and invariant under graph isomorphism.


\subsection{Multiplicity Signature}

The secondary fingerprint channel is motivated by symmetries among vertex deletions.

\begin{observation}
\label{obs:orbit}
Let $G$ be a graph and let $u, v \in V(G)$ belong to the same orbit under $\operatorname{Aut}(G)$.
Then $G \setminus \{u\} \cong G \setminus \{v\}$, so their DRESS fixed points are identical up to the relabeling induced by the automorphism.
In exact arithmetic the sorted fingerprint vectors coincide.  This observation motivates counting repeated rows in the deleted-subgraph DRESS matrix, but we do not identify those row classes here with the full automorphism-orbit partition.
\end{observation}

This gives rise to a purely integer-valued invariant:

\begin{definition}[Multiplicity signature]
\label{def:msig}
Given the $n \times e$ multiset matrix $M$ (where row $i$ is the sorted DRESS fingerprint of $G \setminus \{v_i\}$), the \emph{multiplicity signature} is the sorted tuple of row multiplicities:
\[
\operatorname{msig}(M) = \operatorname{sort}\bigl(\{|\{j : M_j = M_i\}| : i = 1, \ldots, n\}\bigr)
\]
where equality is bitwise (no rounding).
Thus $\operatorname{msig}(M)$ records the multiplicities of repeated deleted-subgraph DRESS rows.
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}
The multiplicity signature is a graph isomorphism invariant.
Because it counts repeated rows within a single graph's deleted-subgraph matrix, it is not exposed to the cross-graph histogram-binning comparison that caused the pooled-histogram collision in Section~\ref{sec:collision}.
\end{proposition}


\subsection{Combined Fingerprint}

The $\Delta^1$-DRESS fingerprint of a graph $G$ is the pair:
\[
F(G) = \bigl(\operatorname{hist}(G),\; \operatorname{msig}(G)\bigr)
\]
Two graphs are declared \emph{separated} ($\Delta^1$-DRESS-distinguishable) if their combined fingerprints differ in either component.
In practice, the histogram alone separated all but one within-family collision case across the full benchmark (Section~\ref{sec:collision}); the multiplicity signature resolved the remaining case.


\section{Experimental Setup}

\subsection{Implementation}

We use the \texttt{dress-graph} library~\cite{castrillo2026dress}, which provides an implementation of $\Delta^k$-DRESS.
The core function computes the $\Delta^1$-DRESS histogram and, optionally, the full multiset matrix.
The experiment runner loads each graph family, computes the combined fingerprint for each graph, and checks for collisions within the family.
Checkpoints are saved atomically every 50 graphs, enabling interruption-safe long-running experiments.
For auditability, the experiments reported here enable multiset retention and save per-graph raw data (sparse histogram and multiset matrix) as compressed files for post-hoc analysis; this extra storage is not required by the fingerprint itself.


\subsection{Parameters}

\begin{itemize}
    \item Deletion depth: $k = 1$ (single vertex deletion)
    \item Convergence tolerance: $\epsilon = 10^{-6}$
    \item Maximum iterations: 100 (convergence was always reached before this limit)
    \item Histogram bins: $\lfloor d^* / \epsilon \rfloor$ for $d^* \in [0, 2]$, giving up to $2 \times 10^6$ bins
\end{itemize}

\subsection{The SRG(40,12,2,4) Collision and Its Resolution}
\label{sec:collision}

During development, the SRG(40,12,2,4) family exhibited a collision in the pooled histogram between two of its 28 graphs: graphs $G_5$ and $G_{25}$ produced identical histograms but are confirmed non-isomorphic by the Bliss canonical labeling algorithm~\cite{junttila2007bliss}.

Investigation revealed the source:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item $G_5$ and $G_{25}$ are both highly symmetric ($|\operatorname{Aut}| = 51{,}840$) but structurally distinct (independence numbers 10 and~7, respectively).

    \item Their DRESS multiset matrices differed by at most $2.2 \times 10^{-16}$, one bit in the mantissa of IEEE~754 double-precision, in a small number of entries.

    \item After histogram binning (with tolerance $\epsilon = 10^{-6}$), these sub-$\epsilon$ differences vanished, producing identical histograms.
\end{enumerate}

Crucially, the row-multiplicity structure differs: $G_5$ has 38 distinct rows (bitwise) in its multiset matrix, while $G_{25}$ has 31.
The multiplicity signature captures this difference as an exact integer invariant: $\operatorname{msig}(G_5) \neq \operatorname{msig}(G_{25})$.

This episode motivated the two-part fingerprint design (histogram + multiplicity signature), which resolved the collision without any change to the underlying DRESS algorithm.
It also illustrates a broader point: \emph{$\Delta^1$-DRESS does distinguish these graphs at the level of raw multiset values}; the collision was an artifact of the histogram quantization scheme, not a limitation of the algorithm's discriminative power.


\subsection{$\Delta^1$-DRESS Strictly Exceeds 3-WL}
\label{sec:3wl-counterexample}

The classical Rook graph $L_2(4)$ and the Shrikhande graph are both SRG(16,6,2,2) and non-isomorphic.
It is well known that the original 3-WL algorithm (the oblivious variant $C^3$) fails to distinguish them~\cite{cai1992optimal}, whereas the folklore 3-FWL ($\equiv$ 4-WL) succeeds.
Since $\Delta^1$-DRESS separates this pair~\cite{castrillo2026dress}, we have:

\begin{theorem}[Empirical]
\label{thm:3wl}
$\Delta^1$-DRESS is strictly more powerful than the original $3$-WL: the Rook $L_2(4)$ and Shrikhande graphs are indistinguishable by $3$-WL but separated by $\Delta^1$-DRESS~\cite{castrillo2026dress}.
\end{theorem}


\section{Analysis}

\subsection{Why \texorpdfstring{$k = 1$}{k=1} Suffices}

The proven theoretical lower bound is Original-DRESS $\geq$ 2-WL~\cite{castrillo2026dress}.
The DRESS--WL Dominance Conjecture~\cite{castrillo2026dress} would extend this to $\Delta^k$-DRESS $\geq$ $(k{+}2)$-WL, but for $k=1$ that comparison remains open.  What Theorem~\ref{thm:3wl} proves directly is the strict separation $\Delta^1$-DRESS $>$ 3-WL.
Furthermore, 3-WL itself does not suffice for all SRG parameter sets: it is known to fail on SRG(16,6,2,2)~\cite{cai1992optimal}.
Across the 34 benchmark families considered here, not a single within-family non-isomorphic pair escapes $\Delta^1$-DRESS.

The key structural insight is that vertex deletion exposes the \emph{local environment} of each vertex in a way that DRESS's edge-level dynamics can fully exploit.
For strongly regular graphs, all vertices ``look the same'' under degree and local counts, but their global embedding in the graph differs.
Deleting a vertex $v$ breaks the local symmetry around $v$'s neighborhood, and DRESS's convergence to a unique fixed point encodes the resulting structural differences as distinct real-valued fingerprints.


\subsection{Computational Cost and Memory}

The cost of a single DRESS run is $\mathcal{O}(I \cdot m \cdot d_{\max})$, where $I$ is the number of iterations (bounded by \texttt{max\_iterations}), $m$ is the number of edges, and $d_{\max}$ is the maximum degree.  Convergence to the unique fixed point is guaranteed by Birkhoff contraction in the Hilbert projective metric~\cite{castrillo2026dress}, so it is natural to separate the numerical iteration count $I$ from the per-iteration work; we do not claim here a graph-independent constant bound on $I$, only guaranteed convergence and the empirical observation that $I$ remained small on the benchmark families tested.
For $\Delta^1$-DRESS the outer loop adds a factor of $n$ (one deletion per vertex), giving $\mathcal{O}(n \cdot I \cdot m \cdot d_{\max})$ per graph, which remains polynomial and practical for graphs of this size.

The asymptotic advantage is stronger on sparse graphs.  For comparison, folklore 3-FWL ($\equiv$ 4-WL), the first WL level sufficient for the Rook/Shrikhande witness, refines all ordered triples and therefore remains $\mathcal{O}(R \cdot n^4)$ time and $\mathcal{O}(n^3)$ memory in the standard tuple-based implementation, regardless of sparsity.  If $m = \mathcal{O}(n)$, then $\Delta^1$-DRESS runs in $\mathcal{O}(I \cdot n^2 \cdot d_{\max})$ total time; for bounded-degree sparse families this simplifies to $\mathcal{O}(I \cdot n^2)$.  In the same regime, streamed memory drops to $\mathcal{O}(n + B)$.

In dense graphs, where $m = \Theta(n^2)$ and $d_{\max} = \Theta(n)$, $\Delta^1$-DRESS becomes $\mathcal{O}(I \cdot n^4)$ total time.  Let $B$ denote the number of histogram bins; for the unweighted benchmark considered here, $B \le 2 \times 10^6$.  The pooled histogram can be accumulated online using $\mathcal{O}(m + B)$ working memory.  The multiplicity signature itself contributes only $\mathcal{O}(n)$ integer storage, so the combined fingerprint occupies $\mathcal{O}(m + B + n)$ space.  The experimental harness used in this paper additionally retains the full deleted-subgraph multiset matrix for post-hoc analysis, which raises working memory to $\mathcal{O}(m + B + n m)$, i.e.\ $\mathcal{O}(n^3)$ on dense graphs.  Duda's edge invariants~\cite{duda2024simple}, the only other method to achieve full separation on the Spence collection, operate on $|E| \times |E|$ matrices, yielding $\mathcal{O}(n^4)$ memory and $\mathcal{O}(n^6)$ time for dense graphs.  Thus the relevant dense-graph comparison is $\mathcal{O}(I \cdot n^4)$ total time for $\Delta^1$-DRESS versus $\mathcal{O}(R \cdot n^4)$ total time for 3-FWL, with $\Delta^1$-DRESS using $\mathcal{O}(n^2)$ streaming memory rather than $\mathcal{O}(n^3)$ tuple storage.


\subsection{Scale of Validation}

The total number of non-isomorphic pairs implicitly resolved by our experiments is:
\[
\sum_{f} \binom{g_f}{2} > 576{,}000{,}000
\]
where $g_f$ is the number of graphs in family $f$ and the sum is over all 16 SRG families.
The dominant contribution comes from SRG(36,15,6,6) with over 529 million pairs.
Together with Duda~\cite{duda2024simple}, who achieved full separation using ad hoc algebraic invariants, this constitutes the most comprehensive empirical validation of polynomial-time graph fingerprinting on hard instances.


\section{Related Work}

\paragraph{Weisfeiler--Leman hierarchy.}
The standard $k$-WL algorithm~\cite{weisfeiler1968reduction, cai1992optimal} refines $k$-tuple colorings and runs in $\mathcal{O}(n^{k+1})$ time and $\mathcal{O}(n^k)$ memory per iteration.
1-WL fails on all SRGs; 2-WL fails on some SRG families but succeeds on others.
3-WL does not suffice for all SRG parameter sets; it is known to fail on SRG(16,6,2,2)~\cite{cai1992optimal}.
Note that 3-WL-equivalent power is available at $\mathcal{O}(n^3)$ time and $\mathcal{O}(n^2)$ memory per round via the folklore 2-FWL ($\equiv$ 3-WL), making full Spence evaluation feasible in principle; however, we are not aware of such an evaluation having been performed.  The first folklore level sufficient for the Rook/Shrikhande witness is 3-FWL ($\equiv$ 4-WL), at $\mathcal{O}(n^4)$ time and $\mathcal{O}(n^3)$ memory per round.
Since $\Delta^1$-DRESS separates the Rook/Shrikhande pair that 3-WL cannot~\cite{castrillo2026dress}, $\Delta^1$-DRESS strictly exceeds 3-WL (Theorem~\ref{thm:3wl}).

\paragraph{Algebraic vertex/edge invariants.}
Duda~\cite{duda2024simple} proposed polynomial-time vertex and edge invariants based on traces and sorted diagonals of powers of neighborhood-restricted adjacency matrices.
These invariants achieved 100\% separation on the Spence SRG dataset (all but 4 pairs resolved by vertex invariants alone, with the remainder resolved by edge invariants).
The method is effective on small graphs ($n \leq 64$), but its cost grows steeply: edge invariants operate on $|E| \times |E|$ matrices, requiring $\mathcal{O}(n^4)$ memory and reaching $\mathcal{O}(n^6)$ per power for dense graphs.
Its relationship to the WL hierarchy has not been formally established.
$\Delta^1$-DRESS, by contrast, scales as $\mathcal{O}(n \cdot I \cdot m \cdot d_{\max})$, has a proven lower bound (Original-DRESS $\geq$ 2-WL), a demonstrated strict separation from 3-WL, and a streamed memory footprint $\mathcal{O}(m + B + n)$ for the combined fingerprint.

\paragraph{Subgraph GNNs.}
Methods such as ESAN~\cite{bevilacqua2022equivariant}, GNN-AK+~\cite{zhao2022from}, and related approaches use vertex-deleted or vertex-marked subgraphs to boost GNN expressiveness beyond 1-WL.
These are supervised methods that learn aggregation functions from data.
$\Delta^1$-DRESS is entirely unsupervised: the aggregation is the deterministic DRESS fixed point, and the comparison is parameter-free.

\paragraph{Babai's algorithm.}
Babai's quasipolynomial-time algorithm~\cite{babai2016graph} solves GI in $\exp(\operatorname{polylog}(n))$ time.
It is a theoretical breakthrough but not practical at this scale.
$\Delta^1$-DRESS is strictly polynomial and runs in seconds per graph.

\paragraph{Canonical labeling.}
Tools like Bliss~\cite{junttila2007bliss}, nauty, and Traces~\cite{mckay2014practical} solve GI by computing canonical forms.
They are practical but worst-case exponential.
$\Delta^1$-DRESS is a one-sided test (it proves non-isomorphism but not isomorphism) that did not fail on any benchmark instance tested in this study.


\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Relationship to the WL Hierarchy}

The proven bound is Original-DRESS $\geq$ 2-WL~\cite{castrillo2026dress}.
The DRESS--WL Dominance Conjecture posits $\Delta^k$-DRESS $\geq$ $(k{+}2)$-WL, but for $k=1$ this remains open.
What Theorem~\ref{thm:3wl} establishes unambiguously is the lower separation
\[
3\text{-WL} \;<\; \Delta^1\text{-DRESS}.
\]
The lower bound ($\Delta^1$-DRESS $> 3$-WL) is concrete: the classical Rook vs.\ Shrikhande pair (SRG(16,6,2,2)) is separated by $\Delta^1$-DRESS~\cite{castrillo2026dress} but not by 3-WL~\cite{cai1992optimal}.
The CFI pair over $K_5$ provides a concrete obstruction at the next WL level: $\Delta^1$-DRESS \emph{fails} on $\text{CFI}(K_5)$, whereas 4-WL distinguishes it~\cite{cai1992optimal, castrillo2026dress}.
Thus one deletion does not subsume 4-WL on all inputs.  This does \emph{not} justify the universal upper bound $\Delta^1$-DRESS $\leq 4$-WL; the exact relationship between $\Delta^1$-DRESS and 4-WL remains open, and incomparability is still possible.

Notably, the separation mechanism on the Rook/Shrikhande pair is the DRESS histogram itself (which already differs between the two graphs~\cite{castrillo2026dress}); on the $G_5$/$G_{25}$ pair from SRG(40,12,2,4), where the histogram collided, it is the multiplicity signature, an integer invariant derived from the row-multiplicity structure of the DRESS multiset matrix.
This continuous-to-discrete pathway (DRESS fixed point $\to$ bitwise row equality within a graph $\to$ row-multiplicity pattern) has no analogue in the WL framework, which operates entirely within discrete colour refinement.


\subsection{Implications for the DRESS--WL Dominance Conjecture}

The conjecture that $\Delta^k$-DRESS $\geq$ $(k{+}2)$-WL~\cite{castrillo2026dress} is now supported by four independent lines of evidence:
(1) the proven base case (Original-DRESS $\geq$ 2-WL),
(2) the CFI staircase experiments, where $\Delta^k$-DRESS empirically matches the $(k{+}2)$-WL boundary for $k = 0, 1, 2, 3$~\cite{castrillo2026dress},
(3) the present work, where $\Delta^1$-DRESS separates all tested SRG families (51{,}718 graphs in total within their respective parameter families), and
(4) the concrete counterexample (Theorem~\ref{thm:3wl}), showing that $\Delta^1$-DRESS already separates a witness missed by 3-WL, so the conjectured $k = 1$ comparison would be \emph{strict} if established in full.

This strict separation from 3-WL is significant because it shows that DRESS captures structural information, specifically, the fine structure of vertex deletions as encoded by the multiplicity signature, that is invisible to the 3-WL colour refinement.

\subsection{Implications for the Reconstruction Conjecture}

$\Delta^1$-DRESS computes a continuous relaxation of the vertex-deletion deck central to the Kelly--Ulam Reconstruction Conjecture~\cite{kelly1942congruence, ulam1960collection}.
Our empirical results provide indirect evidence consistent with the conjecture: the vertex-deletion deck, as captured by $\Delta^1$-DRESS, carries enough information to distinguish all tested graphs.
While this does not prove the conjecture, it adds to the growing body of empirical evidence supporting it.


\subsection{Limitations}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{One-sided test.}
    $\Delta^1$-DRESS can prove non-isomorphism (different fingerprints) but cannot certify isomorphism (same fingerprint does not guarantee isomorphism).

    \item \textbf{Finite precision.}
    The DRESS computation uses IEEE~754 double-precision arithmetic ($\approx$15.7 significant decimal digits).
    The SRG(40,12,2,4) collision illustrates that sub-$\epsilon$ differences can be lost in histogram binning.
    The multiplicity signature mitigates this, but pathological cases may exist.

    \item \textbf{Known obstruction at the 4-WL level.}
    $\Delta^1$-DRESS fails on $\text{CFI}(K_5)$, the CFI pair over the complete graph $K_5$, which requires 4-WL~\cite{cai1992optimal, castrillo2026dress}.
    More generally, CFI graphs over base graphs with treewidth $\geq 4$ require $k > 1$.
    This shows that one deletion does not recover all pairs distinguished by 4-WL; it does not by itself prove the universal upper bound $\Delta^1$-DRESS $\leq 4$-WL.  Higher deletion depths are necessary for at least some harder instances.

    \item \textbf{Scope of hard families.}
    Our benchmark covers all 12 Spence SRG families, four additional SRG families (SRG(45,22,10,11), two subfamilies of SRG(63,32,16,26), and SRG(65,32,15,16)), and 18 constructed hard families, but other hard families may exist.
\end{enumerate}


\section{Conclusion}

We have demonstrated that $\Delta^1$-DRESS, DRESS with a single level of vertex deletion, produces fingerprints that are unique within every tested family across a benchmark of 51{,}816 distinct hard graph instances, including 51{,}718 strongly regular graphs spanning 16 parameter families.
The combined fingerprint (pooled histogram plus multiplicity signature) achieves 100\% within-family pairwise separation, implicitly resolving over 576 million within-family non-isomorphic pairs.

Beyond completeness, we have established a qualitative result: $\Delta^1$-DRESS is \emph{strictly more powerful} than the original 3-WL algorithm.
The classical Rook $L_2(4)$ vs.\ Shrikhande pair (SRG(16,6,2,2)) is non-isomorphic, indistinguishable by 3-WL, but separated by $\Delta^1$-DRESS.
This places $\Delta^1$-DRESS strictly above 3-WL; the CFI staircase experiments in~\cite{castrillo2026dress} further show that $\Delta^1$-DRESS fails on $\text{CFI}(K_5)$, so one deletion does not subsume 4-WL ($\equiv$ 3-FWL).  The exact relationship to 4-WL remains open.

The practical message is clear: $\Delta^1$-DRESS separates every hard benchmark family tested in this study, including the complete Spence SRG collection and additional classical constructions from the GI literature.
The cost is $\mathcal{O}(n \cdot I \cdot m \cdot d_{\max})$ per graph: $\mathcal{O}(I \cdot n^4)$ in dense graphs, $\mathcal{O}(I \cdot n^2 \cdot d_{\max})$ when $m = \mathcal{O}(n)$, and $\mathcal{O}(I \cdot n^2)$ on bounded-degree sparse families.  A streamed implementation of the combined fingerprint uses $\mathcal{O}(m + B + n)$ memory for fixed $\epsilon$, compared with $\mathcal{O}(n^3)$ tuple storage for folklore 3-FWL ($\equiv$ 4-WL); the experiments here retain the full multiset matrix only for post-hoc analysis.

These results provide the strongest empirical support to date for the DRESS--WL Dominance Conjecture ($\Delta^k$-DRESS $\geq$ $(k{+}2)$-WL), and they independently establish a strict separation from 3-WL at $k = 1$.

An open-source implementation is available in the \texttt{dress-graph} library at \url{https://github.com/velicast/dress-graph}.
